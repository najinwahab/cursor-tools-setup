## Post 1
I sat down and thought about what Danny said about bite-sized content the other day. So, let’s clear up the confusion about chunking in AI Search and content strategy:

• 🤖 Chunking isn’t about pleasing search algorithms. It’s about structuring content for real human readers and intelligent retrieval by modern systems. 
 • 🧠 Chunking improves relevance in retrieval-augmented systems by enabling passage-level matching and better semantic understanding. 
 • 🪶 Good structure and UX aren’t mutually exclusive with machine-friendly content. You can serve both audiences at once. 
 • 🪟 The web is shifting from “Google-shaped” to “agent-shaped.” Machines now recombine content across sources, so your content must be legible at the passage level, not just at the page level. 
 • 📊 Better-structured content helps agents retrieve and synthesize more meaningfully, giving you visibility across AI interfaces that go beyond classic search. 
 • 💡 Chunking isn’t a hack. It’s part of future-ready content engineering for the evolving world of AI Search and generative interfaces.

## Post 2
I get the whole "it's just SEO" makes y'all feel comfortable, safe, and confident in a time where things are confusing and change is coming at you from all directions. After all, who wants to admit they don't know what's going on or what to do? 

That argument makes you look silly in the eyes of people that control your future. It's an indication that you don't understand how business works and you're limiting your own opportunities. These are commonly entrenched problems that the SEO community generally has that are showing up glaringly now. 

But, I got time today, let's get into your arguments:

Point: "Tell me what do you do differently for GEO that you don't do for SEO?"
Counterpoint: This is perhaps the most basic and corniest of gotchas that I see used. Yes, there is a high overlap between tactics that for legacy search and AI surfaces -- FOR NOW. This is quickly becoming less true with better understanding of query fan out, the advent of memory/hyper-personalization, integration of agents, direct feeds, and software integrations. 

BUT even if it is true, it doesn't matter. The channels treat the inputs differently and there is a different value exchange in AI Search. 

Consider this: PR, link building, fundraising, and outbound sales all employ the same tactics. You build a list, write some form letters, send a bunch of emails, and keep going until you get the outcomes you want. Yet, no one is conflating those as the same thing. Those are often 4 different teams and 4 different budget line items in the same company. That's because, while the surfaces are exactly the same, the value exchange is different. 

The "it's just SEO" argument is cutting you off from this maturity within a business. C-level people understand this. You do not.

Point: *AI Search uses retrieval, so SEO matters more than ever"
Counterpoint: I don't know how many ways I can say it. SEO has not kept up with advances in retrieval. Most of the major legacy SEO tools are giving you insights and data based on old paradigms that don't align with the information retrieval state of the art.

This argument is reflective of an idealistic form of SEO that very few people are actually doing. It's Mostly "Python SEOs" because they can build for the gaps. I'd argue these those people are engineers, but frankly, until the software catches up to the SOTA, it requires more than SEO to do SEO. To be clear, this has been my position since before the genAI revolution.

Finally, the data indicates that optimizing the same way for retrieval is not enough. ZipTie.dev's data from May showed being in the top 10 gave you a 25% chance of being a source for AI surfaces. New data from Profound indicates a 19% overlap between Google Search and ChatGPT visibility. Just this week Josh Blyskal shared data reinforcing that it's less about position and more about relevance. 

So, if you're happy with a 20% hit rate, then sure, it's just SEO. While we're here, tell me more about how sand tastes too.

## Post 3
I've been thinking about how valuable an embeddings index of the web will be in the AIO/AI Mode era.

It would be a lot easier to identify synthetic queries that query fan out is using. 🤔

...or at least those that are being captured in rankings data.

Here's how it would work:

1. Take the chunks from the citations in the AIO or AI Mode response.
2. Search for the nearest neighbor chunks in the embeddings index.
3. See what keywords those URLs rank for.
4. isolate the topical cross-section in the rankings.

That would get you a list of passages you compete against ranked by semantic similarity, the keywords they rank for ranked by how many citations rank for those keywords, and then you could experiment with Gemini to reverse engineer the reasoning steps by having it simulate why it chose what it chose.
