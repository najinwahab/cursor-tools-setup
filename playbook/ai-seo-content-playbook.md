## The Core SOP: Step-by-Step Workflow

### Step 1: Build a Topical Authority Map (Before Writing Anything)
### Step 1: Build a Topical Authority Map Before Writing Anything

Before writing a single article, map out the full territory of your subject. The goal is not to
write one excellent piece — it is to become, over time, the most comprehensive and
consistently reliable source on your topic in Google's eyes.

Koray Tugberk Gubur explains this using what he calls a **Root → Seed → Node** structure.
The Root is your broad subject (for example, "AI-powered SEO"). Seeds are the major
subtopics within it (content production, keyword research, link building, AI tools). Nodes are
the specific articles that cover each subtopic in depth. Every page links to related pages with
unique anchor text — creating a connected web of knowledge rather than isolated articles.

The results can be dramatic. In his foundational case study, a website grew from zero to
128,000 organic visits in just 123 days — with no backlinks and no traditional technical SEO
— simply by covering a topic with deep, connected, expert content.

*(Source: Koray Tuğberk Gübür — "Importance of Topical Authority for SEO," Holistic SEO
Digital, February 11, 2021 — https://www.holisticseo.digital/theoretical-seo/topical-authority/)*

**My assessment:** I find this framework genuinely convincing — when we include broad,
deep coverage of a subject, it signals to Google that we are not a one-time contributor but an
ongoing expert in that niche. However, I recognise a practical limitation that Koray's critics
also raise: this approach requires sustained research effort over weeks or months before a
single article is published. More importantly, once a large content cluster is built, keeping it
accurate becomes a significant editorial burden — if one fact becomes outdated or a new
consensus emerges, every related article in the cluster may need revision. This makes the
approach more realistic for teams than for solo writers.

**How to apply this in practice (simplified version):**
1. Pick your core topic and write it at the top of a blank document
2. Below it, list every question someone might ask about that topic — aim for at least 20
3. Group similar questions together — each group becomes a "Seed" category
4. Each individual question within a group becomes a future article ("Node")
5. Start writing from the most fundamental questions first — the ones everything else depends on
6. In every article, link to at least two or three related articles in your cluster
